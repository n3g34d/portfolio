Eye Tracking

Eye tracking investigates the effect of web design on immediate visual focus.

Overview

The goal of this project is for UI/UX students to evaluate different user interface designs and interpret the results of qualitative user tests. In this eye tracking project, a group of three students and I, created two different web design concepts for a fictional bakery. One was vibrant and colorful while the other was calmer and relaxed. We hypothesized that users who view both versions would spend more time viewing the images of the vibrant design and more time reading the words of the more relaxed design. This hypothesis was then tested using eye tracking technology and our results were analyzed. The purpose of this project was for us to gain experience creating alternative web designs, perform a common A vs B design experiment, and learn to interpret the results of the experiment.

Process

Below I've summarized my four step project process. Click through the slides to learn more!

Step 1: Formulating a Hypothesis

Our first step in the process was to formulate a hypothesis regarding user preference of these designs. We were provided an initial template of a bakery homepage and asked to create a hypothesis about user interaction with two different versions of this page.

We hypothesized that a more vibrant page would result in a viewer spending more time looking at images versus text. Alternatively, a more relaxed, calm page would direct the user's attention towards the text. We chose this hypothesis as we thought the result would be somewhat unpredictable. We were genuinely curious if changing the color scheme and theme of a site could redirect user attention.

With this hypothesis, we decided to construct two pages: one vibrant and one calmer. See below for the initial homepage we were instructed to change and draw our hypothesis from:

Step 2: Redesigning Two Bakery Pages

Once we had created a hypothesis, we used Figma to redesign our pages. Figma is a collaborative user interface tool that allows users to create detailed, realistic mockups of their web designs. Our mockups needed to align with the goal of testing our hypothesis so we split our group in half and created a vibrant and relaxed page.

The sub-group that created the vibrant page made the following changes:

  • High contrast in text color
  • Various, loud fonts
  • High saturation photos in the center of the screen
  • Large item description fonts overlayed on the image

The sub-group that created the relaxed page(my sub-group) made the following changes:

  • Calm earthy colors as a background
  • Low saturation images
  • Elegant font
  • Neat, organized layout

Step 3: Conducting Eye Tracking Experiment

In the next step, we found two friends to participate in our user testing. We scheduled a time to use the eye tracking software in the CIT and began our experiment together as a group.

The process went as follows:

  • User 1 sat in a chair facing the computer while a eye tracking camera scanned their eyes
  • User 1 was then asked to close their eyes and was given the following intructions : "On the count of three open your eyes and examine the website. Decide what you want to order and say done when you’ve completed the task"
  • A group member then pulled one of our Figma designs on the computer screen and counted to three
  • User 1 then opened their eyes and performed the task. The User said "done" when they had finished and then we halted data collection.

Overall, this process was exciting. We found it surprising that a special camera contained the ability to accurately track the user's eyes on a screen. It seemed as though our participants also enjoyed this process!

Step 4: Analyzing the Data

The images to the right show our images with a heatmap of where our users eye’s spent the most time. You'll see that both users spent most of their time looking at images. From this qualitative data, we found no significant difference between the two heatmaps in terms of time spent examining images.

Here is our conclusion:

We hypothesized that the user on redesigned page A(vibrant) would spend more time looking at images, whereas the user on page B(calm) would mostly spend time reading text. Our findings from the replay and heatmap generated disproved this, as both users seemed extensively focused on their respective page’s images. Ultimately, minimal attention was given to the text on both versions of the page.

Conclusion

Overall, this project provided a great introduction to A/B testing: the process where two versions are presented to users to examine user preference. We enjoyed the creativity involved in developing themed bakery Figma pages and the opporunity to interact with new technology. Although this experiment yielded no significant results, we were very pleased with the experience.
